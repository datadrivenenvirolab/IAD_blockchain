CONTRIBUTION ESTIMATED AND DETERMINED AT THE NATIONAL LEVEL

INDC-Honduras

National Context

Due to its geographic location in the widest part of the Central American isthmus, the Republic of Honduras is permanently exposed to extreme weather events that are being exacerbated by climate change. The biophysical and topographic characteristics of the country, as well as the levels of poverty, confers a high vulnerability to events of this nature. Therefore, in the study for 2015 of the Global Climate Risk Index of German watch, the Republic of Honduras is identified as the country most affected worldwide by extreme weather events in the period 1994-2013.

For the Republic of Honduras, with a percentage of emissions of greenhouse gases less than 0.1% of the world total, the priority undoubtedly is adaptation to climate change. However, as this document demonstrates, the country is also committed to supporting the fight against climate change, under the principle of common but differentiated responsibilities, and to contribute to mitigation measures. The Republic of Honduras is also committed to the adoption of a new legally binding agreement applicable to all Parties, and it hopes that under this new legal instrument, the global average temperature increase will be limited to 1.5 ºC.

At the national level, the Climate Change Law, the National Climate Change Strategy and the Agroforestry Law for Rural Development, the latter in the process of approval, articulate the public policy in several fundamental axes aimed at having a low carbon and resistant development. To the effects of climate change that promotes adaptation and brings co-benefits to the population. This vision promotes the transformation of society into a culture of sustainable production and consumption, which manages equity, efficiency, and protection of the environment and natural resources with equity and efficiency, and promotes an adequate adaptation to climate change. This poses a huge challenge given the national circumstances, territorial conditions and environmental characteristics that add up to and overlap with the challenges of climate change.

The Republic of Honduras believes that policies and measures to combat global warming should focus on the "human face" of climate change. To this end, the actions to be taken must improve the living conditions of people who, because of their situation, may be more vulnerable to climate change. These actions should ensure a cross-cutting perspective of human rights and gender equity, ensuring that women, indigenous peoples and Afro-Hondurans have a full and effective participation in decision-making. This message of hope is already included in the policies of the Republic of Honduras within the strategic plan of government "Plan of All for a Better Life".

Finally, it should be noted that the Nationally Estimated and Determined Contribution of the Republic of Honduras has been designed based on national capacities, expected financing conditions and national circumstances.

Means of implementation
Financing

The Republic of Honduras has initiated the preparation of an Investment Plan on the subject of climate change. Through which, it is sought to mobilize climate financing to achieve the objectives established in the INDC, in addition to those that will be established in the National Adaptation Plan.

This Investment Plan arises from the financing and institutional strengthening needs identified through a diagnosis of the current capacities and limitations in the country to access and manage climate funds. A review of public spending in terms of climate change is also being prepared and will serve as input to the Climate Change Investment Plan. It has also been found that the Republic of Honduras can hardly meet these needs without external support, given the limited resources available to the country
Technology transfer The Republic of Honduras is currently undertaking a technology needs assessment that will serve as a basis of the creation of the Technological Action Plan
Support for Capacity building One of the four pillars of the Climate change Directorate of the environment Secretariat is knowledge management. General Perspective

The Republic of Honduras recognizes the different effects of climate change on different vulnerable groups of people. It also believes that the gender perspective is an essential aspect to be included in a sustainable national development model. Therefore, it recognizes the role of women as an agent of fundamental change, which must be taken into account in decision-making that allows the development of a society low in carbon and resistant to the effects of climate change.

Adaptation

The Republic of Honduras considers adaptation to climate change as a priority to reduce the vulnerability of the country. There are also opportunities to promote mitigation measures and actions that also increase the adaptive capacity of their populations, as well as their natural and productive systems. All this is reflected in the General Law on Climate Change and the National Strategy on Climate Change, which describes actions and plans and actions to protect, conserve and restore marine and terrestrial coastal ecosystems and their biodiversity; Comprehensive risk management and sector vulnerability.

Loss and damage

Due to its geographical location, the Republic of Honduras is permanently exposed to extreme hydro meteorological events. In addition to the above, the biophysical and topographic characteristics of the country, despite deriving from an abundant natural wealth, give it a high vulnerability to events of this nature. This situation is exacerbated by the high levels of degradation to which natural systems are subject, mainly because of poorly planned human interventions and the lack of awareness of the populations around the management of these resources, among other factors.

The devastating impact of Hurricane and Tropical Storm Mitch in October 1998 led to the loss of more than 14,000 lives, more than half a million victims, the loss of 20 years of investments in road and water infrastructure, 3.8 billion (Equivalent to 70% of the GDP of that year) and the fall of the productive apparatus of the country, plunging the Republic of Honduras into a crisis whose sequels persist until the present. Although extreme phenomena such as Mitch and Hurricane Fifi in 1974 are the referents of the physical, environmental, economic and social vulnerability of the country, the high incidence of recurrent phenomena that, year after year, Causing the loss of lives, housing, infrastructure and means of production. These recurring phenomena dramatically affect the development of the country and create eternal circles of "Investment-reconstruction" that constitute a permanent erosion of the state economy and private initiative.

Climate change tends to exacerbate the incidence, magnitude and frequency of extreme hydro meteorological events and their adverse effects, causing greater pressures on existing natural and human systems. Honduras faces the adverse effects of climate change that have manifested themselves in the form of a higher incidence of hurricanes, torrential rains and floods, drought heat waves, rising temperatures, decreased precipitation, water depletion, Land and crops, rising sea levels, and increasing tropical diseases. Likewise, the greater frequency and magnitude of the climatic variability phenomena such as "El Niño" and "La Niña", relevant aspects that are anticipated, will impact more intensely in the coming decades.

It should be noted that in addition to the exposure to which the national territory is subject to the events / phenomena referred to, the low adaptive capacity and high sensitivity of the population, derived from the high percentage of this population that is under the national poverty line, Other factors, contributes to the fact that during the last years the Global Climate Risk Index of the Germanwatch organization places Honduras as one of the countries most affected worldwide by the adverse effects of climate change.

Adaptive Measures

In the Republic of Honduras, adaptation to climate change has gained greater prominence in recent years, opening up space within a country agenda that until very recently had been dominated almost entirely by climate change mitigation and carbon markets. The sectors of the country identified as priorities by the National Climate Change Strategy (ENCC) are:

  • Water resources
  • Risk management
  • Agriculture, Soils and Food Security
  • Forests and biodiversity
  • Coastal marine systems
  • Human health
  • Infrastructures (especially hydropower)

The Republic of Honduras has made good progress in establishing a favorable regulatory and institutional framework to work towards reducing its vulnerability to climate change, as demonstrated by, inter alia, the National Climate Change Strategy, the Climate Change Law, The National Strategy for Food and Nutrition Security, the National Action Plan to Combat Desertification, Strategies for Adaptation to Climate Change for the Agrifood Sector, Health and Coffee Industry. Currently, a series of strategic climate change planning processes are under way, coordinated by the National Directorate of Climate Change, among which are: National Action Plan on Climate Change, Technological Needs Assessment, Change Investment Plan Climate, National REDD + Strategy and the National Adaptation Plan.

Currently, the National Adaptation Plan is being developed and it is expected that its inputs will provide updated and detailed information on vulnerability, in order to prioritize and focus future adaptation efforts. An advance of this plan is anticipated for the end of 2015, reason why the measures contained within this INDC are partial. However, the final document of the National Adaptation Plan foreseen for 2016 will collect the inputs provided by other climate change strategic planning processes currently in the process of generation, including the INDC.

The agri-food sector in the Republic of Honduras is one of the sectors of greater economic importance and at the same time one of the most vulnerable to climate change. Given the importance of this sector, its adaptation measures have been prioritized. Among those identified in the National Strategy for Climate Change, the following stand out:

  • Change in agricultural practices: introduction of agroforestry systems "Quesungual"; Reduction of fertilizer load; Use of slow-acting organic fertilizers; Changes in crop calendars; Encouraging the production of Creole seeds adapted to local conditions; Introduction of insect repellent plants into established crops; Modification or elimination of inappropriate agricultural burning practices; Measures to combat erosion; Micro-irrigation programs in slope agriculture; Practices of biological control of pests and diseases; Development of organic fertilization systems; stimulus and / or inductive impulse to organic agricultural production, including fiscal and financial incentives
  • Changes in livestock practices: changes in pasture time; Sowing of improved pastures; Implementation and diffusion of intensive livestock under housing; and, limitation of the burning of pastures for the control of mites in cattle.
  • Capacity building and research: selection and development of varieties and species of crops and pastures resistant to droughts, floods and major; Implementation of the national integrated pest management program; Design and implementation of a national biodiversity research program; Research and development of natural biocides; Promotion of the establishment of regional research centers and a national outreach program; And, development of sustainable systems based on agroecology.
  • Society and legislation: improvement of land tenure; Diversification of national agricultural production and, in particular, subsistence agriculture; Strengthening of the country's food security policies and strategies; Improvement of the systems of storage, processing and preservation of agricultural production; And, development of model farms for the dissemination of best cultivation practices.

Finally, another sector prioritized for its relevance at the national level is the marine-coastal sector. The Republic of Honduras has a territorial area of ​​112,492 km2 and an Exclusive Economic Marine Zone of 226,955 km2. The Caribbean coast of Honduras, which is part of the Great Marine Ecosystem of the Caribbean, is approximately 650 km long. The Mesoamerican Reef - which extends from Mexico to Belize, Guatemala and Honduras, as well as three groups of islands: The Bay Islands and the Cayos Cochinos Archipelago; The Keys and Banks Miskitos; and the smaller Swan Islands. The Bay Islands group comprised of Roatan, Utila, Guanaja and the Cayos Cochinos has one of the best reefs and is fundamental for the development of the country's tourism. These islands are surrounded by coral reefs that support important fisheries. The northern coast of Roatan enjoys an almost continuous coral reef. In addition to coral reefs, there are other characteristics of the marine-coastal ecosystem that are equally essential to their health and productivity. These include mangroves, wetlands, seagrass beds and sandy beaches. Coastal and marine ecosystems in the Republic of Honduras face threats associated with climate change. Increasing water temperature increases the frequency of coral bleaching events, while sea level rise affects coral photosynthesis by reducing the amount of light coming to them, also causing flooding and retreating at the limit of Mangroves and increased erosion caused by waves. On the other hand, climate change scenarios suggest changes in wind speeds and precipitation volumes, among other factors, favoring conditions that will favor fishing, however, if measures are not taken, this could result in an envelope -Exploitation of the fishing resource, additional to the current one.

Because of the economic importance they represent for the country, as the main source of livelihood for indigenous and Afro-Honduran groups and other coastal communities, and because of their high vulnerability, it is essential to implement strategies that promote / to maintain the integrity and functioning of these ecosystems.
